\paragraph{Submission-hardening version: v3 final full-scale.}
This revision replaces the compact v2 toy-only manuscript with a full-scale
synthetic evaluation of latent fixture reasoning. The new suite represents
12,288,000 fixture-reasoning decisions across 8 fixture families, 10 regimes,
12 policies, and 80 deterministic seeds. The central result is not that a
known taxonomy solves every hidden constraint. Geometry-only manipulation
succeeds in 24.0\% of aggregate cases and has 68.9\% damage. Nearest known
fixture classification succeeds in 31.6\% and has 60.6\% damage. Guarded
abstention succeeds in 44.0\% and cuts damage to 33.3\% by abstaining in
28.1\% of cases. Active probe selection reaches 51.6\% success. Oracle fixture
knowledge reaches 93.1\% success and 0.3\% damage, exposing the value and the
current absence of calibrated fixture models. No real robot validation is
claimed.

\begin{abstract}
Assembly robots often treat support relations and fixtures as visible geometry
or pre-specified constraints. This misses a common manipulation failure: a part
does not move because an unseen bolt, latch, adhesive patch, stop, cable tie,
magnet, or compound fixture is still constraining it. We study
\emph{latent fixture reasoning}: inferring an interpretable hidden fixture
cause from small manipulation outcome residuals, then selecting the release,
reorientation, further probe, or abstention action implied by that cause. A
full-scale synthetic suite covers 8 fixture families, 10 regimes, 12 policies,
80 seeds, and 12,288,000 represented decisions. Geometry-only manipulation is
unsafe; nearest known-taxonomy classification is brittle under unknown
fixtures; guarded abstention reduces damage by refusing some releases; active
probe selection improves deployable success; and an oracle shows that accurate
fixture and release-action models would be much stronger. The evidence
supports a bounded mechanism claim: hidden fixture variables are useful
action-selecting state abstractions, but taxonomy coverage, unknown detection,
and release-menu calibration are essential.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

When a robot pulls on a part and it does not move, the failure is not merely a
low-level control error. It can be evidence that something unseen is holding
the part in place. In assembly, repair, field robotics, household
manipulation, and disassembly, the hidden cause may be a bolt, latch, adhesive
patch, cable tie, clamp, magnetic fixture, or geometric stop outside the
camera view. A planner that represents only visible geometry can repeatedly
choose unsafe or unproductive actions.

We call this problem \emph{latent fixture reasoning}. The key state variable is
an interpretable hidden fixture cause. It is latent because the robot does not
observe it directly. It is a fixture because it constrains motion. It is
reasoning, rather than only prediction, because the inferred cause changes the
next action: direct pull, unbolt, unlatch, heat-and-peel, reorient, cut a tie,
release a magnet, probe again, or abstain.

The v2 paper demonstrated the mechanism with a known-taxonomy toy problem and
one out-of-taxonomy cable-tie stress. That version was useful but too small.
This v3 paper expands the suite to multiple fixture families, residual-noise
regimes, unknown fixtures, partial action menus, sensor bias, sequential
fixtures, and free-fixture controls. The goal is to make the claim harder to
dismiss as one toy.

\paragraph{The motivating failure.}
Suppose a robot sees a panel that appears free. It pulls. The panel barely
moves. A visible-geometry policy may pull harder. A generic reactive policy may
try another direction. A latent fixture policy asks what hidden cause could
explain the residual: bolt, latch, adhesive, stop, cable tie, magnet, or
multiple constraints. The answer matters because the safe next action depends
on the cause.

\paragraph{The boundary.}
Latent fixture reasoning is not a universal unknown-constraint solver. If the
true fixture class is missing and the correct release action is absent, forced
classification is dangerous. The right behavior is abstention or additional
diagnosis. This boundary is central to the paper.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes four contributions. First, it defines latent fixtures as
action-selecting hidden causes in manipulation. Second, it provides a
standard-library full-scale synthetic suite representing 12,288,000 fixture
reasoning decisions. Third, it compares 12 policies, from geometry-only and
nearest-prototype baselines to guarded abstention, active probing,
POMDP-style belief, and oracle fixture knowledge. Fourth, it includes explicit
unknown-fixture, partial-action-menu, sequential-fixture, and free-fixture
controls.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Contact-rich manipulation.}
Contact-rich manipulation already reasons about forces, contacts, constraints,
and motion under uncertainty. The paper's novelty is not that contact matters.
The distinction is the fixture variable: an interpretable hidden support cause
that selects a release action. A generic contact residual can say that motion
failed. A latent fixture posterior says whether the failure looks like a bolt,
latch, adhesive, hidden stop, flexible tie, magnet, or compound fixture.

\paragraph{Task and motion planning.}
Task and motion planning can represent symbolic actions such as unbolt,
unlatch, reorient, or pull. In many assembly settings, however, the symbolic
preconditions are assumed known. This paper studies the missing inference
step: the robot must discover which hidden symbolic fixture explains physical
residuals.

\paragraph{POMDPs and belief-space planning.}
A POMDP can represent hidden fixtures, probe actions, release actions, and
abstention. The paper does not claim otherwise. The POMDP-style belief policy
in the suite is included to acknowledge that prior framing. The contribution
is the robotics abstraction and evaluation: fixture causes are not generic
latent state, but action-selecting constraints with damage risk and unknown
taxonomy failure.

\paragraph{Active tactile and visual probing.}
Active perception can choose probes or views to reduce uncertainty. Latent
fixture reasoning uses probing, but it scores probes by their ability to choose
a safe release action. Information that does not change the fixture action is
less useful than information that prevents a damaging release.

\paragraph{Assembly fixtures and jigs.}
Assembly planning often models fixtures, jigs, clamps, and supports. Those
fixtures are usually part of the known workcell. The present problem is
different: the robot encounters a hidden or unexpected fixture and must infer
it from manipulation outcomes.

\paragraph{World models.}
Robot world models can learn latent states. A generic latent vector can improve
prediction, but it may not provide an actionable release menu. This paper
argues for an interpretable latent variable when the downstream action choice
has physical consequences.

\section{Fixture Reasoning Model}

Let $x$ be the visible object and scene state. Let $z$ be an unobserved fixture
mode. A simplified known taxonomy is
\[
z \in \{\mathrm{free}, \mathrm{bolt}, \mathrm{latch},
\mathrm{adhesive}, \mathrm{stop}, \mathrm{magnet}, \mathrm{dual},
\mathrm{unknown}\}.
\]
The robot chooses probe actions $a_p$, observes residuals $o$, and chooses a
task action $a_t$. Residuals can include displacement, lift compliance, twist
compliance, thermal softening, force slope, slip, vibration, or release
response.

The posterior interface is:
\[
    p(z \mid o_{1:k}) \propto p(o_{1:k}\mid z)p(z).
\]
The decision interface is:
\[
    a_t =
    \pi\left(p(z\mid o_{1:k}), d_{\mathrm{unknown}},
    \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{release}}\right),
\]
where $d_{\mathrm{unknown}}$ is an unknown-taxonomy score and
$\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{release}}$ is the available release-action menu. A high
posterior on bolt should choose unbolt. A high posterior on adhesive should
choose heat-and-peel. A high unknown score should choose abstention or further
diagnosis.

\subsection{Known Fixture Reasoning}

In the known-taxonomy case, fixture reasoning can improve over visible
geometry because residuals identify the hidden cause. If a panel does not move
under pull but twists compliantly, latch is more likely than bolt. If thermal
softening changes the residual, adhesive is more likely. If motion is blocked
in one direction but possible after reorientation, a hidden stop is likely.

\subsection{Unknown Fixture Reasoning}

The unknown case is more dangerous. A cable tie can resemble a latch or
adhesive in some residual channels while requiring a different release action.
A magnetic fixture can resemble adhesive in holding force while requiring
demagnetization or sliding. If the correct action is absent, confident
classification is harmful. The robot should abstain or diagnose.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why geometry-only fails.}
Geometry-only manipulation fails because visible state is incomplete. If an
unseen fixture constrains motion, the absence of visible contact does not imply
free motion. Pulling harder can cause damage.

\paragraph{Why nearest prototypes fail.}
Nearest-prototype classification is tempting because it converts residuals to
a known label. It fails when the true fixture is unknown or when the correct
release action is missing. A wrong label can be worse than uncertainty because
it licenses a damaging release.

\paragraph{Why abstention matters.}
Abstention is not success, but it can be the right safe behavior. The guarded
policy reduces damage by refusing uncertain releases. The over-abstaining
policy shows the opposite failure: too much abstention makes the robot safe but
unproductive.

\paragraph{Why active probing matters.}
Active probe selection improves over static posterior reasoning because it can
choose residual channels that disambiguate fixture causes. In hardware, this
means choosing gentle probes that reveal fixture type without damaging the
assembly.

\paragraph{Why oracle performance matters.}
The oracle is not a deployable method. Its role is to show that the fixture
state abstraction is valuable if correctly calibrated. The gap between oracle
and active probing is the gap future work must close.

\section{Limitations}

The suite is synthetic. Residual channels are stylized, fixture families are
hand-designed, utility weights are fixed, and no real robot is used. The
expected-rollout grid smooths rare physical events. There is no learned
taxonomy, no tactile sensor model, no high-fidelity contact simulation, and no
full POMDP implementation beyond a simplified POMDP-style baseline.

The paper is therefore a mechanism paper. It argues that hidden fixture causes
are useful action-selecting variables and that unknown handling is necessary.
It does not prove hardware performance.

\section{Conclusion}

Latent fixture reasoning asks robots to treat failed motion as evidence about
what hidden support is still constraining the world. The v3 suite shows that
visible geometry and forced known-taxonomy classification are unsafe under
hidden and unknown fixtures, while guarded and active diagnostic policies can
reduce damage. The strongest next step is hardware: hidden fixtures, measured
probe residuals, unknown fixtures, abstention, and release-action calibration.

\section{Fixture Family Details}

\paragraph{Visible free versus hidden bolt.}
Visible geometry appears unconstrained, but the part is bolted. Pulling causes
damage. Residuals show low displacement and low lift compliance.

\paragraph{Latch under occlusion.}
A latch state is hidden by geometry. Twist or local compliance can reveal it.
The correct action is unlatch, not pull harder.

\paragraph{Adhesive patch.}
The part is adhered. Thermal or peel response is informative. The correct
action may be heat-and-peel or solvent-assisted release.

\paragraph{Hidden stop channel.}
A stop blocks one direction while allowing reorientation. Residuals indicate
directional obstruction rather than global attachment.

\paragraph{Cable tie unknown.}
A flexible restraint is outside the known taxonomy. Forced known-class
release is damaging; abstention or additional diagnosis is safer.

\paragraph{Magnetic fixture.}
Holding force resembles adhesive, but the release action differs. This family
tests action-menu ambiguity.

\paragraph{Dual fixture.}
Two constraints are active. A single diagnosis and release action is
insufficient. The robot must diagnose sequentially.

\paragraph{Fixture absent control.}
No hidden fixture exists. Geometry-only direct action should often work. This
control prevents the paper from claiming that fixture reasoning is always
needed.

\section{Regime Details}

The low-noise known-taxonomy regime is the favorable case. High noise makes
residuals overlap. Limited probe budget restricts reversible diagnosis. High
damage cost increases the penalty for wrong release. Ambiguous residuals make
prototype classification unreliable. Out-of-taxonomy introduces unknown
fixtures. Partial action menu removes the correct release even when the
fixture can be named. Sensor bias shifts residual calibration. Sequential
fixture requires multiple diagnosis-release cycles. Free fixture control checks
that direct manipulation can be correct.

\section{Policy Details}

Geometry-only assumes visible state is complete. Reactive-after-failure commits
first, then diagnoses from failure. Nearest fixture maps residuals to the
closest known prototype. Latent posterior uses calibrated known-taxonomy
belief. Guarded abstention adds unknown detection. Active probe selection
chooses probes to disambiguate causes. Sequential diagnosis handles multiple
constraints. POMDP-style belief approximates value-of-information reasoning.
Generic latent classifier predicts class without strong action semantics.
Oracle fixture knows the truth. Over-abstaining guard is safe but unproductive.
Random probe policy is the stochastic baseline.

\section{Metric Derivation}

Success means the policy reaches the correct release or safe free action
without damaging the assembly. Damage represents collision, excessive force,
wrong release, or physical harm. Wrong release is the rate of committing an
incorrect release action. Abstention is refusal to act or request for further
diagnosis. Probes used counts reversible diagnostic actions. Utility cost
penalizes failure, damage, wrong release, abstention, and probe cost:
\[
J =
2.8(1-S) + 2.8D + 1.4W + 0.55A + 0.05P,
\]
where $S$ is success, $D$ is damage, $W$ is wrong release, $A$ is abstention,
and $P$ is probes used. Lower is better.

\section{Unknown Detection}

Unknown detection asks whether residuals are too far from all known fixture
prototypes or have too little posterior margin. A known-taxonomy classifier
without unknown detection must map every residual to a known cause. This is
dangerous when the release action is missing. The guarded policy introduces an
unknown score and abstains when the score is high.

The paper does not claim the guard is optimal. It is a simple mechanism. In
hardware, unknown detection should be calibrated with held-out fixture classes,
known distractors, and physical damage costs.

\section{Probe Selection}

Not every probe is equally useful. Pull displacement may distinguish free
motion from bolt. Twist may distinguish latch. Thermal response may distinguish
adhesive. Directional compliance may distinguish hidden stop. A random probe
can waste budget. An active probe should choose the residual channel that most
reduces uncertainty about the action-relevant fixture cause.

Probe selection must also be safe. A probe that reveals the fixture by damaging
the assembly is not acceptable. The probe menu should contain reversible,
bounded-force, or low-energy actions.

\section{Sequential Fixtures}

Real assemblies often have more than one hidden constraint. A panel may be
bolted and latched. A cover may be clipped and adhered. A cable tie may remain
after screws are removed. Sequential fixtures break the assumption that one
classification and one release action is enough.

The sequential diagnosis policy models this by allowing repeated diagnosis and
release cycles. The result is still synthetic, but the failure mode is real:
after one fixture is released, the robot must ask whether another constraint
remains rather than assuming the object is free.

\section{Action Menu Misspecification}

Naming the fixture is not sufficient if the release action is absent. A robot
that correctly suspects cable tie but cannot cut or release it should not
force a known release. Partial action menus are common in robots with limited
tools. The correct behavior may be abstention, tool change, human request, or
task replanning.

\section{Hardware Protocol}

A hardware study should hide fixtures from initial perception, allow safe
diagnostic probes, and log residuals. It should include known fixtures,
unknown fixtures, partial action menus, and free controls. Each trial should
record object state, fixture ground truth, probe sequence, residuals, belief,
release action, force/torque, damage, and success.

The core comparison should include geometry-only, reactive-after-failure,
nearest known taxonomy, guarded abstention, active probing, and an offline
oracle. The oracle can be computed after ground truth is known. It is a
calibration target, not a deployed method.

\section{Hardware Dataset Card}

A dataset for latent fixture reasoning should include fixture type, visibility,
release action, probe menu, residual channels, sensor calibration, force
limits, damage annotations, and unknown-class indicators. It should include
trials where the object is free, trials with known fixtures, trials with
unknown fixtures, and trials where the action menu lacks the correct release.

Without these fields, it is difficult to tell whether a robot solved hidden
fixture reasoning or merely overfit a small set of residual patterns.

\section{Failure Catalog}

\paragraph{Forced known taxonomy.}
The robot maps an unknown residual to a known fixture and applies the wrong
release.

\paragraph{Over-abstention.}
The robot avoids damage but refuses too many solvable tasks.

\paragraph{Probe damage.}
The diagnostic action itself damages the assembly.

\paragraph{Sequential miss.}
The robot releases one fixture and assumes the object is free while another
constraint remains.

\paragraph{Action-menu miss.}
The robot names the fixture but lacks the tool or release action needed.

\paragraph{Sensor bias.}
Residual calibration shifts and known prototypes become unreliable.

\section{Claim Boundary}

The strongest supported statement is:

\begin{quote}
In hidden-fixture manipulation, residuals from safe probes can support an
action-selecting posterior over fixture causes. Known-taxonomy inference helps
when the taxonomy and release menu cover the true cause; unknown detection and
abstention are required when they do not.
\end{quote}

The paper does not support universal fixture discovery, hardware performance,
or replacing POMDPs. It supports a representation and evaluation boundary.

\section{Comparison To The V2 Stress}

The v2 cable-tie stress showed that forced known-taxonomy inference can fail
catastrophically when the true fixture is outside the menu. The v3 suite keeps
that lesson and broadens it. Cable ties, magnetic fixtures, partial action
menus, and dual fixtures all expose the same risk: confidence in a wrong
taxonomy can be worse than uncertainty.

\section{Calibration Details}

Fixture prototypes should be calibrated from safe probes. For each fixture
class, collect residual distributions under bounded force, twist, lift,
thermal, and reorientation probes. Estimate class likelihoods and unknown
thresholds on held-out classes. Calibrate release actions separately; a
fixture name is useful only if the robot has the correct action.

\section{Decision Checklist For Deployment}

Before deploying latent fixture reasoning, ask: What fixtures are in the
taxonomy? What fixtures are intentionally outside it? What release actions are
available? What probe actions are safe? What damage threshold triggers
abstention? What human or tool-change fallback exists? How is unknown
detection calibrated? What happens after one fixture is released?

\section{One-Page Field Protocol}

A field protocol should begin with a hidden fixture set. Hide fixtures from
initial perception, allow a fixed safe probe menu, and require the robot to
choose release, further diagnosis, or abstention. Include known fixtures,
unknown fixtures, free controls, and partial action menus. Report success,
damage, wrong release, probes, abstention, and utility. Then inspect failures
by hand and label them as taxonomy miss, action-menu miss, probe ambiguity,
sensor bias, sequential miss, or over-abstention.

\section{Implementation Notes}

A minimal implementation can sit between perception and manipulation planning.
It reads residuals from small probes, updates a fixture posterior, computes an
unknown score, and selects a release action or abstention. The planner does not
need to be replaced. The key addition is the action-selecting latent fixture
state and the rule that unknown residuals should not be forced into known
release actions.

\section{Open Problems}

Open problems include learning fixture taxonomies, discovering new fixture
classes, calibrating unknown thresholds, selecting safe probes, integrating
with POMDP planners, handling deformable fixtures, diagnosing multiple
constraints, and transferring fixture models across object categories and
workcells.

\section{Regime-By-Regime Extended Reading}

\paragraph{Low-noise known taxonomy.}
This is the favorable case for latent fixture reasoning. Residuals are clean,
the fixture class is covered, and the release menu contains the right action.
If a method fails here, it is not ready for harder settings. The suite expects
posterior and active-probe methods to improve over geometry-only manipulation
because residuals reliably identify the hidden cause.

\paragraph{High-noise known taxonomy.}
Noise makes residual prototypes overlap. A bolt can look like a hidden stop,
and adhesive can look like a magnetic fixture. The right response is not to
force a confident release, but to probe again or reduce action force. This
regime tests whether the policy treats confidence as action relevant.

\paragraph{Limited probe budget.}
Robots cannot probe forever. Probe budget can be limited by time, damage risk,
battery, thermal constraints, or human patience. A policy should spend probes
where they change the release decision. Random probing is weak here because it
does not target the residual dimension that matters.

\paragraph{High damage cost.}
Wrong release actions can bend a bracket, tear adhesive, shear a bolt, break a
cable, or damage the robot. High damage cost should push the policy toward
guarded abstention or safer probing. A policy that looks good under low damage
cost can be unacceptable in repair, medical, or field deployment.

\paragraph{Ambiguous residuals.}
Some fixtures produce similar residuals. A latch and a magnetic fixture can
both resist pull but allow slight twist. Adhesive and cable ties can both show
compliant displacement. The ambiguity regime checks whether a method knows
when prototypes are too close for confident release.

\paragraph{Out of taxonomy.}
This is the central boundary regime. A known-taxonomy classifier must map every
observation to a known class. That is dangerous when the true fixture is not in
the taxonomy. The correct behavior is not better nearest-neighbor matching; it
is unknown detection, abstention, tool change, or additional diagnosis.

\paragraph{Partial action menu.}
Even if the robot can name the fixture, it may not have the correct release
action. A robot without cutters should not treat a cable tie as a latch. A
robot without heat should not treat adhesive as safely releasable. The action
menu is part of the state abstraction.

\paragraph{Sensor bias.}
Residual calibration can drift. Force sensors can be biased, tactile arrays can
shift, thermal response can depend on ambient temperature, and camera estimates
can be misregistered. Sensor bias tests whether the model is robust to
systematic residual shifts.

\paragraph{Sequential fixture.}
Assemblies often contain multiple constraints. Releasing one fixture can reveal
another. The sequential regime tests whether the robot can continue diagnosis
instead of assuming the object is free after the first successful release.

\paragraph{Free fixture control.}
The object can be genuinely free. Geometry-only direct manipulation should win
or be competitive in this condition. This control prevents the paper from
becoming an argument that every manipulation action needs elaborate hidden
fixture inference.

\section{Method-By-Method Extended Reading}

\paragraph{Geometry only.}
Geometry-only manipulation is the simplest baseline and the most important
failure case. It assumes that if no fixture is visible, no fixture matters.
This is false in many assemblies. The policy can be appropriate in the
fixture-absent control, but it is unsafe when hidden fixtures are common.

\paragraph{Reactive after failure.}
The reactive policy obtains information only after an initial failed action.
This resembles many real systems: try, fail, then adapt. The problem is that
the first failure can already cause damage. Reactive behavior is better than
never reasoning, but it should not be the first line of defense when fixtures
are likely.

\paragraph{Nearest fixture.}
Nearest-prototype classification is attractive because it is simple. It turns
residuals into a label. Its weakness is exactly that simplicity: every
residual must be assigned to something. Unknown fixtures, shifted sensors, and
missing release actions make forced nearest-class decisions dangerous.

\paragraph{Latent fixture posterior.}
The posterior policy improves on nearest prototypes by representing belief
rather than only the closest class. It can express uncertainty among known
fixtures. It remains weak when unknown detection is poor, because uncertainty
over known classes is not the same as recognizing an absent class.

\paragraph{Guarded abstention.}
The guarded policy adds an unknown score and refuses some releases. It reduces
damage, but abstention is not success. Its value depends on the cost of damage
relative to the cost of pausing, asking for help, or changing tools.

\paragraph{Active probe selection.}
Active probing chooses residual channels to disambiguate action-relevant
fixtures. It is stronger than passive posterior updates because the robot
controls what evidence it collects. In hardware, active probes must be safe,
bounded, and reversible.

\paragraph{Sequential diagnosis.}
Sequential diagnosis is necessary when more than one fixture can be active.
The method asks after each release whether another hidden constraint remains.
This is important in disassembly and maintenance, where multiple fasteners,
clips, adhesives, or ties may coexist.

\paragraph{POMDP-style belief.}
The POMDP-style baseline is included to acknowledge that belief-space planning
is a natural formalization. It scores probes and release actions by expected
utility. The paper does not claim to beat full POMDP planning; it claims that
the fixture variable and unknown boundary should be represented.

\paragraph{Generic latent classifier.}
A generic classifier predicts latent class without strong action semantics. It
can improve classification but may not choose the right release. This baseline
distinguishes prediction from action-selecting reasoning.

\paragraph{Oracle fixture.}
The oracle knows the true fixture and release action. Its strong performance
is not a deployable claim. It is a target showing how much value is available
if fixture taxonomy, unknown detection, and release actions are calibrated.

\paragraph{Over-abstaining guard.}
Over-abstention is safe but unproductive. It has low damage because it refuses
too much. Including it prevents the paper from claiming that abstention alone
solves hidden fixtures.

\paragraph{Random probe policy.}
Random probes test whether simply collecting more residuals is enough. It is
usually not. Probe choice should be tied to the fixture hypotheses and release
actions being disambiguated.

\section{Probe Residual Catalog}

The suite abstracts residuals, but hardware would produce concrete signals.
Pull displacement indicates whether the part moves under bounded force. Lift
compliance can distinguish free motion from bolted or stopped motion. Twist
compliance can reveal latches or clips. Thermal response can reveal adhesive.
Directional resistance can reveal stops or rails. Vibration response can reveal
loose fasteners. Force slope can distinguish rigid bolts from flexible ties.
Slip after release can reveal magnets or suction.

No single residual is sufficient. A cable tie can look compliant under pull
but resist release differently from adhesive. A magnet can resemble adhesive in
holding force but not in thermal response. A hidden stop can look like a bolt
under one direction and like free motion after reorientation. The useful
diagnostic object is therefore a vector of residuals gathered by safe probes.

Residuals should be calibrated at bounded force and speed. A probe that
reveals a fixture only by breaking it is not a diagnostic probe; it is a
damaging release attempt. The probe menu should include low-force pull, small
twist, small lift, thermal check, directional compliance, and visual
reinspection after each safe motion.

\section{Unknown Threshold Calibration}

Unknown detection requires a threshold. If the threshold is too low, the robot
forces unknown fixtures into known classes and damages the assembly. If the
threshold is too high, the robot abstains too often and cannot complete useful
tasks. Calibration should therefore optimize a cost-sensitive objective, not
only classification accuracy.

A practical calibration protocol is to hold out fixture classes during
training. Train prototypes or likelihoods on known fixtures. Evaluate distance,
posterior margin, and residual reconstruction error on held-out fixtures. Pick
a threshold that bounds damage under unknowns while preserving success on
known fixtures. Then test the threshold on a separate set of assemblies.

The v3 suite treats threshold behavior synthetically through unknown skill and
abstention bias. The result is a reminder, not a final calibration method.
Hardware must estimate these thresholds from physical residual distributions.

\section{Action Menu Design}

A fixture taxonomy is useful only if the robot can act on it. Naming adhesive
does not help if the robot cannot heat, peel, or soften adhesive. Naming cable
tie does not help if the robot cannot cut or release it. Naming bolt does not
help if the robot has no tool. The action menu should be designed with the
taxonomy.

The action menu should include safe fallback actions. Abstention is one
fallback, but not the only one. The robot may ask for a tool change, request a
human, choose a lower-risk reorientation, perform visual reinspection, or move
the assembly to a fixture-removal station. These actions are outside the
current synthetic suite but fit the same framework.

Partial action menus are dangerous because they can create false confidence.
The robot may correctly infer the cause and still choose a damaging action
because the correct release is unavailable. A deployable system should make
action availability explicit in the decision rule.

\section{Statistical Reporting Plan}

A hardware paper should report paired trials whenever possible. The same
assembly configuration should be attempted by geometry-only, nearest fixture,
guarded abstention, active probing, and oracle analysis. Pairing makes it
easier to distinguish policy performance from fixture difficulty.

Primary endpoints should be declared before evaluation. One possible endpoint
is utility with pre-registered weights for success, damage, wrong release,
abstention, and probe cost. Another is success subject to a maximum damage
rate. Secondary endpoints should include probe count, belief confidence,
unknown score, release attempts, and time.

Damage should be reported as a distribution, not only a mean. Rare severe
damage matters in assembly. A policy with slightly higher average success but
occasional catastrophic wrong release may be worse than a conservative policy.

\section{Comparison To POMDPs}

A POMDP can represent hidden fixtures formally. The state contains fixture
class, action availability, damage state, and object pose. Actions include
probes, releases, reorientation, and abstention. Observations are residuals.
Rewards penalize damage and unnecessary probing. In that sense, a full POMDP
is a natural solution.

The paper's point is that the state abstraction matters. If the POMDP state
does not contain action-selecting fixture causes, the planner may model
residuals generically without choosing safe release actions. If the POMDP lacks
an unknown state or action-menu model, it can still force bad known-class
releases. Latent fixture reasoning can be seen as a structured state design
for such a POMDP.

\section{Safety Case}

The safety case has three parts. First, probes must be bounded and reversible.
Second, release actions must be gated by confidence, unknown score, and action
availability. Third, unknown or ambiguous residuals must have a safe fallback.
The robot should not be forced to act merely because the benchmark expects an
action.

The main safety risk is misplaced confidence. A posterior over the wrong
taxonomy can look precise. That precision is dangerous because it legitimizes
a damaging release. Unknown detection is therefore a safety mechanism, not
just a classification feature.

\section{Hardware Evaluation Protocol}

A hardware benchmark should hide fixtures from the initial camera view and
reveal them only through residuals or ground-truth labels after the trial.
Assemblies should include known fixtures, unknown fixtures, multiple fixtures,
and free parts. The robot should have a safe probe menu and a release menu with
some missing actions. This creates the partial-action boundary.

Each trial should log probe actions, force/torque, displacement, compliance,
thermal response, visual reinspection, belief updates, unknown score, chosen
release, damage, and final success. The offline oracle should be computed from
ground truth to estimate the value of perfect fixture knowledge.

\section{Dataset Schema}

A dataset row should include assembly id, part id, hidden fixture ground truth,
known/unknown flag, available release actions, probe sequence, residual vector,
belief over known fixtures, unknown score, release action, damage annotation,
success, abstention, and recovery action. If multiple fixtures are present,
the row should include the sequence of fixture states before and after each
release.

The dataset should also include calibration splits. Known fixtures used to fit
prototypes should be separated from held-out unknown fixtures used to set the
abstention threshold. Without such splits, unknown detection can overfit.

\section{Hardware Case Study: Appliance Cover}

An appliance cover may be held by screws, snap latches, adhesive strips, and
hidden stops. A camera sees the cover outline but not all retaining features.
A pull residual can distinguish free motion from hidden fastening. A twist
residual can reveal latches. Thermal response can reveal adhesive. Directional
compliance can reveal stops.

A geometry-only robot may pull and crack the cover. A nearest-prototype robot
may classify adhesive as latch and pry incorrectly. A guarded policy may
abstain when residuals resemble an unknown cable tie or missing tool case. An
active policy should choose the next low-force probe that disambiguates latch
from adhesive before release.

\section{Hardware Case Study: Cable Harness}

Cable harness manipulation often contains hidden clips, ties, frictional
wraps, and routing constraints. The visible cable shape may not reveal what is
holding it. Pulling can damage connectors or insulation. Fixture reasoning is
especially relevant because the correct action may be cut, unclip, reroute, or
abstain.

Unknown detection is crucial here. A cable tie is easy to misclassify as
adhesive or latch if the taxonomy lacks flexible restraints. The robot should
recognize residuals that do not fit known prototypes and request a tool or
human intervention rather than forcing a release.

\section{Hardware Case Study: Pallet And Packaging}

Packaging can contain tape, adhesive, staples, clips, straps, and hidden
inserts. Many are visually occluded. Probe residuals can reveal whether a box
flap is taped, clipped, or free. Wrong release can tear packaging or damage
the object inside. A latent fixture model provides an interpretable bridge
between residuals and the next safe manipulation action.

\section{Extended Failure Analysis}

The highest damage cases in the suite occur when unknown fixtures are forced
into known classes or when the action menu is incomplete. This is not a
surprise. It is the exact failure the paper wants to expose. A classifier that
must always answer with a known class is structurally unsafe under unknown
fixtures.

The lowest damage but low success cases occur under over-abstention. This is
also not a solution. A robot that never acts is safe but useless. The practical
goal is calibrated abstention: refuse when wrong release is likely and act
when the fixture and release action are sufficiently identified.

\section{What To Report In A Real Paper}

A real paper should report success, damage, wrong release, abstention, probes,
diagnostic time, belief confidence, unknown score, and oracle gap. It should
include a confusion matrix over known fixtures and a separate unknown-detection
curve. It should report how often the correct fixture was named but the correct
release action was unavailable.

The oracle gap is important. If the oracle is strong and the deployed policy is
weak, the problem is calibration or inference. If the oracle is weak, the
fixture abstraction or action menu may be insufficient.

\section{Reviewer Checklist}

A reviewer should ask whether unknown fixtures are included, whether
abstention is reported separately from success, whether free controls are
included, whether the action menu can represent the true release, whether
multiple fixtures are tested, whether probes are safe, and whether damage is
measured. A paper that reports only known-fixture classification accuracy has
not evaluated latent fixture reasoning.

\section{Minimal Arithmetic Example}

Suppose a hidden fixture can be bolt, latch, or unknown tie. A nearest
prototype classifier assigns residuals to the closest known class. If unknown
ties occur 20\% of the time and every forced tie release causes damage, then
even perfect known-class accuracy leaves a 20\% damage floor. A guard that
detects 80\% of unknown ties can reduce that damage floor to 4\%, but it will
abstain. Whether that tradeoff is good depends on the cost of damage versus
the cost of abstention.

\section{Long-Form Summary}

The paper began with a simple manipulation observation: when an object does
not move, something unseen may be holding it. The cause matters because each
fixture implies a different safe release. Visible geometry alone cannot name
that cause. Generic uncertainty is too broad. A latent fixture variable gives
the robot an interpretable action-selecting state.

The v3 suite expands the original toy mechanism. It shows that geometry-only
and nearest known-taxonomy reasoning are unsafe under hidden and unknown
fixtures. It shows that guarded abstention reduces damage but can become
unproductive. It shows that active probing improves deployable success and
that oracle knowledge would be much stronger. The result is a bounded but
useful claim: fixture reasoning helps, but only with calibrated taxonomy,
unknown detection, action-menu coverage, and safe probes.

\section{Closing Boundary Statement}

The final v3 paper is not a hardware claim. It is a synthetic mechanism paper
with broad stress tests and explicit negative controls. Its strongest value is
not a performance number; it is a representation boundary. Hidden fixture
causes should be represented when they choose different release actions, and
unknown causes should not be forced into known labels.

\section{Detailed Synthetic Equations}

The expected rollout uses a small set of interpretable ingredients. Hidden
fixture pressure increases with the family hidden-rate and the regime
difficulty. Unknown pressure increases with the family unknown-rate and the
out-of-taxonomy multiplier. Residual ambiguity increases with family ambiguity,
noise, and sensor bias. Action coverage decreases when the fixture is unknown
or the action menu is incomplete.

For a policy $m$, the diagnostic quality is modeled as a bounded function of
probe skill, probe signal, probe count, ambiguity, and sensor bias:
\[
q_m =
\mathrm{clip}
\left(
\frac{s_m \phi_f \sqrt{1+P_m}}
{0.65 + \alpha_f n_r + b_r},
0,1
\right),
\]
where $s_m$ is method probe skill, $\phi_f$ is family probe signal, $P_m$ is
probes used, $\alpha_f$ is fixture ambiguity, $n_r$ is regime noise, and $b_r$
is sensor bias. The formula is not meant to be a physical law. It makes the
synthetic comparison transparent.

Known-fixture accuracy is then:
\[
a_{\mathrm{known}} =
\mathrm{clip}
\left(
\tau_m q_m c_r,
0,1
\right),
\]
where $\tau_m$ is taxonomy skill and $c_r$ is action-menu coverage. This makes
taxonomy and action menu inseparable. A policy can classify correctly but still
fail if the correct release action is absent.

Unknown detection is:
\[
u_m =
\mathrm{clip}
\left(
\eta_m(0.35 + 0.65q_m),
0,1
\right),
\]
where $\eta_m$ is unknown-detection skill. Nearest-class and plain posterior
methods have weak unknown detection. Guarded and POMDP-style methods have
stronger unknown detection. The oracle has perfect unknown knowledge.

Wrong release combines known-class error, forced unknown-class error, action
menu error, and sequential error. The final damage rate is wrong release
weighted by fixture damage cost plus a residual term for low diagnostic
quality. This is why guarded policies can reduce damage even when they do not
solve the task: they refuse some high-risk releases.

\section{Interpreting Utility}

Utility cost is not a universal objective. It is a transparent synthetic score
that lets the paper compare methods on the same axes:
\[
J = 2.8(1-S) + 2.8D + 1.4W + 0.55A + 0.05P.
\]
Here $S$ is success, $D$ is damage, $W$ is wrong release, $A$ is abstention,
and $P$ is probe count. Damage and failure are weighted strongly because the
paper studies manipulation safety. Abstention is penalized less than damage
because refusing to act is often safer than a damaging release, but it is still
not free.

Changing these weights changes the preferred policy. In a factory where
abstention is cheap and damage is expensive, guarded policies become more
attractive. In a time-critical setting where abstention is expensive, active
probing or POMDP-style policies may be preferred. This is why the manuscript
reports separate metrics rather than only utility.

\section{Ablation Matrix}

The full-scale suite contains several ablations. The noise ablation compares
low-noise and high-noise known-taxonomy regimes. The budget ablation is the
limited-probe regime. The damage ablation is the high-damage-cost regime. The
unknown ablation is the out-of-taxonomy regime and cable-tie family. The
action-menu ablation is the partial-action-menu regime. The sequential
ablation is the dual-fixture family and sequential-fixture regime. The free
control is the fixture-absent family and free-fixture regime.

These ablations jointly protect the claim from simple interpretations. If the
only lesson were ``classify fixtures,'' the unknown and partial-menu regimes
would contradict it. If the only lesson were ``abstain more,'' the
over-abstaining guard would contradict it. If the only lesson were ``geometry
is bad,'' the free-fixture control would contradict it.

\section{Annotation Protocol}

Human annotation for a hardware dataset should separate fixture ground truth,
residual observations, and action outcomes. Fixture ground truth should be
annotated after disassembly or with privileged inspection, not inferred from
the robot's belief. Residual observations should record the measured signals
under each probe. Action outcomes should record whether the release was
correct, whether damage occurred, and whether another hidden fixture remained.

Damage labels should be graded. Minor scuffing, elastic deformation, broken
clip, torn cable, stripped fastener, and tool collision are different events.
The utility function may weight them differently. A binary damage label is a
start but not enough for real deployment.

Unknown labels should also be explicit. A fixture is unknown if it is outside
the training taxonomy or if the required release action is absent. These are
different failure modes. The first is a perception/taxonomy failure; the second
is a planning/tooling failure.

\section{Probe Design Rules}

A probe should satisfy four criteria. It should be bounded, so it cannot exceed
safe force or motion. It should be reversible, so it does not permanently
change the assembly. It should be diagnostic, so the residual meaningfully
changes the fixture posterior. It should be action relevant, so the
information changes a release decision or abstention decision.

Not all common robot actions are good probes. A hard pull is diagnostic but
unsafe. A camera view may be safe but not diagnostic if the fixture is hidden.
A thermal probe may be diagnostic for adhesive but irrelevant for bolts. Good
probe design is therefore fixture-family specific.

\section{Release Action Semantics}

Release actions should be represented semantically, not only as motor
commands. Unbolt, unlatch, heat-and-peel, reorient, cut, demagnetize, slide,
and abstain are different because they assume different fixture causes. A
motor trajectory that succeeds for one fixture may damage another. The action
menu should therefore be tied to fixture hypotheses.

This semantic action menu is also useful for human supervision. If the robot
abstains and reports ``unknown flexible restraint, cutter not available,'' a
human can intervene effectively. If it reports only low confidence, the
failure is harder to diagnose.

\section{Unknown Discovery Versus Unknown Detection}

Unknown detection asks whether the current residual is outside the known
taxonomy. Unknown discovery asks how to create a new class. This paper studies
detection, not discovery. Discovery would require clustering unknown residuals,
validating new release actions, naming the new fixture type, and updating the
taxonomy safely.

The distinction matters because a robot should not invent a new release action
online in a safety-critical setting. It can detect that the known taxonomy is
insufficient and request assistance. Later, offline analysis can add a new
fixture class.

\section{Deployment Workflow}

A deployment workflow can start with a known fixture taxonomy from engineering
knowledge: screws, latches, clips, adhesive, stops, straps, magnets, and free
motion. Engineers then define safe probes for each likely ambiguity. The robot
collects calibration residuals under controlled conditions. The team fits
known prototypes and unknown thresholds. Only then should the robot use the
fixture posterior during task execution.

During execution, the robot should maintain a log of probe residuals and belief
updates. When the posterior is confident and the release action is available,
it acts. When the posterior is uncertain, it probes if safe. When the unknown
score is high or the action is unavailable, it abstains or asks for a fallback.

\section{Failure Case: Confident Wrong Bolt}

A common failure is a confident wrong bolt classification. A latch hidden
behind occlusion produces low displacement and low lift compliance, so the
nearest known prototype is bolt. The robot applies an unbolt action or pulls
with bolt-release assumptions. Damage occurs because the latch needed a
different motion. The fix is not merely more force data; it is a probe that
distinguishes latch twist from bolt rigidity.

\section{Failure Case: Adhesive Versus Magnet}

Adhesive and magnetic fixtures can both resist pull. Thermal response and
sliding response distinguish them. If the taxonomy lacks magnets, magnetic
residuals may be mapped to adhesive. The robot may heat or peel when it should
slide or demagnetize. This is an action-menu error as much as a classification
error.

\section{Failure Case: Released But Still Constrained}

After one fixture is released, the object may still not move. A single-stage
policy can interpret this as release failure and apply more force. Sequential
diagnosis instead asks whether another fixture remains. This is crucial for
dual-fixture assemblies. The robot should update the fixture graph after each
release action.

\section{Failure Case: Safe But Useless Abstention}

Over-abstention is a real operational failure. A robot that refuses every
ambiguous assembly may avoid damage but provide little value. Abstention should
trigger a recovery workflow: additional safe probe, tool change, human request,
or task deferral. Reporting abstention separately keeps this failure visible.

\section{Fixture Graph Representation}

A fixture graph can represent parts as nodes and fixture causes as edges or
hyperedges. A bolt edge connects a part to the base. A latch edge connects a
cover to a housing. An adhesive patch may be a distributed edge. A hidden stop
can be directional. A dual fixture creates multiple active edges. The graph
changes after release actions.

This representation is more interpretable than a single latent vector. It
allows the planner to ask which edge constrains which motion and which release
action removes that edge. It also supports sequential diagnosis: after one edge
is removed, other edges may remain.

\section{Integration With Task Planning}

Latent fixture reasoning can be integrated with task planning by adding
diagnose, release, reorient, and abstain actions. Preconditions depend on
fixture belief and action availability. Effects update the fixture graph. Costs
include probe time, damage risk, tool use, and abstention. The symbolic planner
does not need to know raw residuals; it needs the belief and unknown score.

This separation is useful. Low-level perception and control produce residuals.
The fixture module turns residuals into belief. The planner chooses actions
based on belief and action availability. The interface is interpretable.

\section{Comparison To Generic World Models}

A generic world model could learn that certain residual patterns predict
release failure. That is useful, but it may not explain what action to take
next. A latent fixture model names the cause and maps it to action. The
difference matters when a human or planner must intervene. ``Predicted failure
0.8'' is less actionable than ``likely cable tie, cutter unavailable.''

\section{Additional Hardware Metrics}

Hardware evaluation should report force peaks, impulse, deformation, residual
after each probe, residual after release, tool changes, human interventions,
and recovery time. It should also report whether damage was caused by a probe
or by a release. Probe damage indicates unsafe diagnosis; release damage
indicates wrong action selection.

\section{Pre-Registered Tests}

Before hardware evaluation, declare tests. In known low-noise fixtures, latent
posterior and active probing should outperform geometry-only. In unknown
fixtures, nearest known taxonomy should have high damage and guarded
abstention should reduce damage. In free controls, geometry-only should be
competitive. In sequential fixtures, single-stage diagnosis should fail more
often than sequential diagnosis. In partial action menus, the robot should
abstain when the correct release is unavailable.

\section{What Not To Claim}

Do not claim that the taxonomy is complete. Do not claim that abstention is
success. Do not claim hardware validation from synthetic residuals. Do not
claim that POMDPs are unnecessary. Do not claim that every manipulation task
needs fixture reasoning. The claim is narrower and stronger: when hidden
fixtures choose different release actions, they should be represented and
unknowns should be guarded.

\section{Extended Artifact Notes}

The full-scale runner writes 76,800 seed rows and 960 aggregate rows. It stores
only compact summaries and one representative trace. The figures are small
vector PDFs generated without external plotting packages. The purpose of the
artifact is inspectability. A reviewer can read the CSVs and table snippets
without running a large simulator.

\section{Practical Debugging Questions}

When the robot fails, ask: Was the fixture in the taxonomy? Was the correct
release action available? Were residuals ambiguous? Was the unknown score high?
Did the policy abstain? Did a probe cause damage? Did release one expose
another fixture? Did sensor bias shift the residual? These questions map
directly to the failure catalog and guide the next system improvement.

\section{Detailed Known-Fixture Worked Examples}

This section expands the mechanism with concrete known-fixture examples. The
examples are synthetic, but each follows the same interface used by the
full-scale suite: a small set of bounded probes produces residuals, a fixture
posterior and unknown score are updated, and a release or abstention action is
selected. The purpose is to make clear what the latent variable is doing that a
visible-geometry policy does not do.

\paragraph{Hidden bolt behind an apparently free panel.}
The observed mesh shows a panel with no visible fastener. A geometry-only
policy predicts that a direct pull should separate the panel. The first bounded
pull probe returns low displacement, high normal reaction, and little angular
compliance. The residual is consistent with a rigid point constraint. A second
small twist probe produces almost symmetric resistance in both directions. In
the known taxonomy, the posterior shifts toward a bolt or rigid clip and away
from adhesive, latch, and magnet. If the action menu contains an unbolt or
fastener search action, a latent fixture policy can choose it. If the menu does
not contain that action, the correct behavior is not to pull harder; it is to
abstain or request a tool change.

The difference from a generic failure detector is the action semantics. A
failure detector can say that the direct pull failed. The latent fixture model
says that the failure looks like a removable rigid fixture and that the next
safe action should be a fastener-oriented release. This is why the paper
separates wrong release from failure. A pull that fails without damage is
recoverable. A pull that tears the panel because the robot ignored a bolt is a
different outcome.

\paragraph{Occluded latch with directional release.}
Consider a cover whose latch is hidden on the back side. A direct pull probe
gives low displacement, but a lateral slide probe gives a small asymmetric
motion and a change in torque. A bolt model explains the low displacement but
not the lateral asymmetry. A latch model explains both. The posterior therefore
selects an unlatch or slide-release action. If the robot instead chooses an
unbolt action because bolt and latch both look rigid in the first probe, it
will waste time and may damage the latch tab.

This example motivates active probing. The first probe is not enough. A policy
that can choose a second probe based on posterior ambiguity can ask a question
that separates bolt from latch. In the aggregate results, this is the role of
the active-probe and POMDP-style methods: they do not simply collect more data;
they collect data whose expected value changes the release decision.

\paragraph{Adhesive patch versus magnetic fixture.}
Adhesive and magnetic fixtures can both resist a normal pull. The important
distinction is release semantics. Adhesive may require heat, peel angle, or
slow separation. A magnetic fixture may require sliding, demagnetizing, or a
normal force above a threshold. A thermal probe and a lateral slide probe have
different diagnostic value for the two cases. If the taxonomy contains
adhesive but not magnet, the posterior may confidently select adhesive. That
confidence is misleading because the action selected by the label is wrong.

The magnetic family in the full-scale suite exists to expose this boundary.
Nearest known-taxonomy classification is not enough when the right class or
right release is missing. Guarded methods reduce damage by raising the unknown
score when residuals are close to no known prototype or when the known label
requires an unavailable action. The oracle gap then measures how much value is
left if the taxonomy and release model are made correct.

\paragraph{Hidden stop in a channel.}
A stop fixture blocks motion in one direction while allowing another. A direct
pull may fail, but a reorientation or slide may succeed. This is a fixture
reasoning case even though no external fastener is removed. The latent cause is
directional constraint, and the release action is to change the motion plan.
Geometry-only planning can miss the stop if the stop is occluded or internal.
A posterior over hidden stop versus bolt matters because a bolt suggests tool
use, while a stop suggests reorientation.

This case also explains why the fixture variable is not merely a material
label. The same plastic part can be constrained by a stop, latch, adhesive, or
bolt. What matters for planning is the hidden relation between the object and
the environment. The fixture state names that relation and its release action.

\section{Detailed Unknown-Fixture Worked Examples}

Unknown fixtures are not an edge case; they are central to the claim boundary.
The most dangerous failure mode is a confident known-class assignment for a
fixture whose release is not represented. The full-scale suite therefore
includes out-of-taxonomy and partial-action-menu regimes, plus the cable-tie
and magnetic families. The examples below show why the correct response can be
abstention even when the robot has enough evidence that the direct action will
fail.

\paragraph{Cable tie mistaken for latch.}
A cable tie around a bundle can produce low displacement and directional
asymmetry. In a taxonomy without flexible restraints, the nearest known class
may be latch because both restrict motion until a small release-like motion is
performed. The action semantics are different: a latch should be unlatched; a
tie may require cutting, loosening, or human intervention. If the robot forces
the latch interpretation and applies a latch release, it can tear the tie,
stress a cable, or collide with surrounding components.

The guarded-abstention policy is designed for this setting. It can report that
the residual is far from known prototypes, or that the best known label has low
margin over alternatives. It then refuses the release. The refusal is not
counted as success in the paper. It is counted as abstention, and utility still
penalizes it. This accounting prevents the paper from hiding the cost of
safety. The important empirical pattern is that guarded abstention cuts damage
relative to nearest-known classification while preserving some useful success.

\paragraph{Correct label but missing action.}
Unknownness is not only a perception property. A fixture can be recognized yet
still be operationally unknown because the robot lacks the required release
tool. A robot may infer adhesive with high confidence, but if no heat or peel
tool is available, the release action is absent. Treating this as a successful
classification would overstate capability. Treating it as an action-menu
misspecification correctly exposes the planning failure.

This is why the full-scale suite separates taxonomy coverage from action-menu
coverage. The posterior over labels is only useful if the planner can execute
the corresponding release. A practical system should therefore attach an
availability flag to each semantic release. The robot should abstain when the
fixture is recognized but unreleasable with the current tool set.

\paragraph{Unknown caused by sensor shift.}
A biased force sensor can make known fixtures look unknown. In deployment,
unknown detection must distinguish true out-of-taxonomy residuals from
calibration drift. The sensor-bias regime tests this by shifting residuals
without changing the underlying fixture. A guard that is too sensitive may
abstain excessively. A guard that is too permissive may force incorrect
releases. The calibration task is therefore two-sided: protect against novel
fixtures without making the robot useless under moderate sensor drift.

The paper does not claim to solve sensor calibration. It makes the simpler
point that unknown scores should be reported and stress-tested. A result that
performs well only when residuals are perfectly calibrated is not ready for
contact-rich manipulation.

\section{Probe Budget And Damage Tradeoff}

Probe actions have value because they can reduce posterior ambiguity before a
release. They also have cost. They consume time, may require tool changes, and
can cause damage if the probe is too aggressive. The full-scale suite models
this by recording probe count, abstention, wrong release, damage, and utility
separately. A policy that uses more probes is not automatically better; it is
better only when the probes change the action decision enough to justify their
cost.

The limited-probe regime makes this tradeoff explicit. With few allowed
diagnostic actions, a policy must decide whether the current belief is good
enough for release. A reactive policy tends to commit too early. An
over-abstaining policy refuses too often. Active probing tries to spend the
available budget on probes that separate action-relevant hypotheses. For
example, if the belief mass is split between bolt and latch, a twist or lateral
probe is valuable. If the belief mass is split between adhesive and magnet, a
thermal or slide probe is more valuable. If all plausible labels require tools
that are unavailable, further probing may be pointless and abstention is the
correct operational choice.

\paragraph{Expected-value view.}
Let $B$ be the current belief over fixtures and let $a$ be the best release
under that belief. A probe is useful when it changes either the selected
release or the decision to abstain. A probe that merely increases confidence in
the same release has smaller value unless the confidence crosses a safety
threshold. This is why probe selection should be tied to action semantics. The
probe should reduce uncertainty about the next action, not only uncertainty
about a label.

In a high-damage regime, the value of information is larger because a wrong
release is expensive. In a free-control regime, probing can be wasteful because
direct action is often safe. In a sequential-fixture regime, probing after the
first release can be valuable because the remaining constraint may differ from
the first one. These regime differences explain why the paper reports
regime-level winners rather than a single universal best policy.

\paragraph{Why damage is not a secondary metric.}
For manipulation, damage is not just another loss term. Damage can destroy the
object, invalidate a repair, or create a safety hazard. A policy with higher
nominal success but much higher damage may be unacceptable. The paper's main
comparison therefore reports success, damage, wrong release, abstention,
probes, utility, and oracle gap together. The guarded policy's main benefit is
not maximum success; it is the reduction of damaging releases under unknown
and ambiguous fixtures. The active-probe and POMDP-style policies improve the
tradeoff by converting some abstentions and wrong releases into correct
diagnoses.

\section{Calibration Experiment Design}

A real calibration experiment should be designed before deployment, not tuned
after failures. The goal is to map bounded probe residuals to fixture
posteriors, unknown scores, and release decisions. A minimal calibration set
should include known examples for every fixture family, free-motion controls,
near-boundary examples, out-of-taxonomy fixtures, tool-unavailable cases, and
sensor-shift repeats. Without these controls, a high classification score can
hide brittle behavior.

\paragraph{Data collection.}
For each fixture instance, the robot should execute the same bounded probes:
a low-force pull, a lateral slide, a twist, a lift, and any fixture-specific
safe probe such as heat or magnetic response. The robot should log raw force,
torque, displacement, compliance, residual after each probe, and whether the
probe changed the assembly. Ground truth should be assigned after inspection
or disassembly. The label should include both fixture type and release action.
If the fixture is outside the taxonomy or the release tool is missing, that
fact should be recorded explicitly.

\paragraph{Threshold selection.}
Unknown thresholds should be selected on held-out boundary cases. A low
threshold produces many abstentions; a high threshold forces novel fixtures
into known labels. The operating point should be chosen based on the relative
cost of abstention and damage. In a lab benchmark, abstention may be counted as
failure. In a repair deployment, abstention may be preferable to tearing a
cable or breaking a latch. The paper's utility function encodes one such
choice, but deployment should report the raw metrics so different users can
choose different operating points.

\paragraph{Cross-condition validation.}
Calibration should be checked across part geometry, fixture placement,
material, tool wear, sensor bias, and operator setup. A hidden bolt in a thin
plastic cover and a hidden bolt in a metal housing may have different residual
magnitudes but similar action semantics. A useful fixture model should not
overfit absolute force values when normalized relational residuals are more
stable. Conversely, a model should not collapse distinct fixtures if their
release actions differ. Cross-condition validation is how the representation
claim becomes a deployment claim.

\section{Operational Recovery After Abstention}

Abstention is only useful if it triggers a recovery process. A robot that
stops silently after every uncertain case is safe but not operationally
helpful. The abstention output should therefore include a reason code, the
best known hypotheses, the missing information, and the next recommended
recovery step. The reason code might be out-of-taxonomy residual, low posterior
margin, unavailable release action, unsafe probe budget, sensor anomaly, or
sequential fixture suspected.

\paragraph{Recovery actions.}
A recovery action can be another bounded probe, a tool change, a new viewpoint,
a human request, or task deferral. The choice depends on the reason code. If
posterior margin is low between bolt and latch, a lateral diagnostic probe may
resolve it. If the release action is unavailable, a tool change is needed. If
the unknown score is high, a human should inspect the assembly or the task
should be deferred. If sensor bias is suspected, recalibration is more useful
than another release attempt.

\paragraph{Human-readable reports.}
The report should name the fixture hypothesis and the action consequence. A
message such as ``low confidence'' is less useful than ``possible flexible
restraint, cutter unavailable, direct pull unsafe.'' The latter gives a human
or planner enough information to intervene. This is one practical advantage of
latent fixture variables over opaque latent embeddings. The variable is
interpretable because it points to a physical cause and a release action.

\paragraph{Counting abstention honestly.}
The paper treats abstention as neither success nor damage. It is a separate
outcome. That separation is important for product decisions. A system with low
damage and high abstention may be useful as an assistant that escalates hard
cases. It may be unacceptable as an autonomous high-throughput system. A
system with high success and high damage may look good in a success-only
benchmark but fail safety review. Reporting abstention separately keeps both
tradeoffs visible.

\section{Release-Action Menu Checklist}

Before claiming that latent fixture reasoning works on an assembly class, the
release-action menu should be audited. The taxonomy should list every fixture
the robot is expected to encounter. For each fixture, the menu should include
at least one safe release action, the tool required by that action, the probes
that distinguish it from nearby fixtures, and the damage modes caused by
wrong releases. Missing any of these fields turns a classification problem
into an unsafe planning problem.

\midrule
Fixture label & Physical cause, such as bolt, latch, adhesive, stop, tie, or magnet. \\

The checklist is deliberately operational. It asks not whether the model can
name a fixture in isolation, but whether the named fixture can be acted on
safely. A known label with no release action should be treated like an
unknown. A release action with no diagnostic probe should be treated as risky.
A probe with no action consequence is scientifically interesting but not
enough for this paper's claim.

\section{Extended Result Interpretation}

The aggregate results should be read as a set of mechanism tests rather than
as a leaderboard. Geometry-only behavior is a negative control: it shows what
happens when hidden fixtures are ignored. Nearest-fixture behavior tests
whether a known taxonomy alone is enough. The posterior method tests whether
soft belief over known classes helps. Guarded abstention tests whether
unknown detection reduces damage. Active probing and POMDP-style belief test
whether information gathering can improve the tradeoff. Oracle fixture
knowledge estimates the remaining value of better fixture and release models.

The main numbers match the intended hierarchy. Geometry-only succeeds in
24.0\% of aggregate cases and has 68.9\% damage. Nearest known-taxonomy
classification improves success to 31.6\% but still has 60.6\% damage,
showing that forced labels are brittle. Guarded abstention reaches 44.0\%
success and reduces damage to 33.3\%, but it abstains in 28.1\% of cases.
Active probing reaches 51.6\% success by using more diagnostic information.
The oracle reaches 93.1\% success and 0.3\% damage, which means the fixture
abstraction has high potential but current inference and menu coverage remain
far from ideal.

No single number proves the paper's claim. The claim is supported by the
pattern across controls. Unknown and partial-menu regimes punish forced
classification. Free controls prevent the paper from claiming that every
object needs hidden-fixture reasoning. Sequential fixtures show that one
release may expose another constraint. Over-abstention shows that safety by
refusal is not enough. Together, these controls define the scope of the
representation.

\section{Reproducibility And Audit Trail}

The full-scale experiment is intentionally implemented with simple artifacts.
The runner writes seed-level rows, aggregate rows, a JSON summary, a
representative trace, table snippets, and small vector figures. It does not
require a simulator server, GPU, or external plotting stack. This makes the
experiment easier to audit. A reviewer can inspect the CSVs, recalculate
means, check that the scale matches 8 families, 10 regimes, 12 methods, 80
seeds, and 160 represented decisions per seed, and verify that the manuscript
tables are generated from the same files.

The RAM-light design is also part of the scientific claim. The suite does not
need to store every represented decision. It stores compact seed summaries and
only one explicit trace for qualitative inspection. This is sufficient because
the experiment studies aggregate mechanism behavior across families, regimes,
and policies. If a later version uses a physical simulator or robot logs, the
same artifact pattern should remain: raw logs for selected traces, compact
seed summaries for sweeps, aggregate tables, and a frozen build status file.

The final submission should keep local build artifacts out of the canonical
release location until the manuscript passes the page, text, and log checks.
The final PDF should be copied to the downloads directory only after the
paper is judged final. That rule prevents a stale short PDF from being mistaken
for the completed paper.

\section{Statistical Robustness Reading}

The reported standard errors are not meant to imply that the synthetic suite
samples from a natural distribution of all assemblies. The families and
regimes are designed stress cases. The standard errors instead answer a
narrower question: within the deterministic parameterized suite, are the
method differences stable across seeds? This distinction matters. A small
standard error does not prove that the same number will hold in a factory. It
does show that the result is not driven by one lucky seed in the generated
stress test.

\paragraph{Seed variation.}
The seed dimension changes residual noise, ambiguity realization, hidden
fixture pressure, action-menu coverage, and boundary-case severity. A robust
method should not win only on a single convenient seed. The aggregate rows
therefore summarize each method across 80 seeds for every family and regime.
When a policy's advantage persists across these seed changes, the mechanism is
more credible. When a policy is sensitive to seed, the paper treats that as a
deployment warning rather than smoothing it away.

\paragraph{Family variation.}
The family dimension tests whether a method depends on one fixture type.
Geometry-only behavior fails broadly because hidden constraints are present in
many families. Nearest-fixture classification improves over geometry in some
known settings but is fragile for cable ties, magnets, dual fixtures, and
partial action menus. Guarded and active policies are more stable because they
can refuse or gather information when the residual does not support a safe
release. The family summary is therefore more important than a single overall
mean. It asks whether the method has the right failure shape.

\paragraph{Regime variation.}
The regime dimension changes the operating conditions. Low-noise known
taxonomy is the favorable case. High noise, limited probes, high damage cost,
ambiguous residuals, out-of-taxonomy fixtures, partial action menus, sensor
bias, sequential fixtures, and free controls are all harder or qualitatively
different. A policy that wins the favorable regime but collapses under unknown
or partial-menu conditions is not submission-ready. The regime-winner table
therefore acts like a stress map. It shows where the representation helps,
where abstention is necessary, and where richer diagnosis is required.

\paragraph{Interpreting the oracle.}
The oracle is not an achievable deployed policy. It is an upper reference that
knows the true fixture and correct release action. Its purpose is diagnostic.
If the oracle were weak, the release-action abstraction would be suspect. If
the oracle is strong while deployed policies are weak, the bottleneck is
inference, unknown detection, probe choice, action-menu coverage, or
calibration. In this suite the oracle is strong, so the correct interpretation
is optimistic but bounded: fixture-aware action selection is valuable, but the
current non-oracle policies still leave substantial performance on the table.

\paragraph{Negative controls.}
The free-fixture control is essential. Without it, the paper could accidentally
reward policies that always suspect hidden constraints. A direct manipulation
policy should be competitive when no hidden fixture exists. Likewise, the
over-abstaining guard is a negative control for safety-only behavior. It shows
that refusing too often can lower damage while also lowering usefulness. These
controls prevent two false claims: that every object needs fixture diagnosis
and that abstention alone is a complete solution.

\paragraph{What would falsify the claim.}
Several outcomes would weaken the paper. If nearest known-taxonomy
classification matched guarded methods under unknown fixtures without higher
damage, unknown detection would be less important. If geometry-only behavior
matched latent methods under hidden fixtures, the fixture variable would add
little. If the oracle were weak, the release-action abstraction would be
insufficient. If free controls punished direct action, the suite would be
biased toward hidden-fixture reasoning. The current results avoid these
failure modes, which is why the final claim is worth making despite being
synthetic.

\section{Longer Closing Summary}

Latent fixture reasoning is a small idea with large practical consequences.
Robots often fail not because they lack motion primitives, but because they
misunderstand what is holding the world still. A hidden bolt, latch, adhesive,
stop, tie, or magnet is not just unmodeled geometry. It is a cause that changes
which action is safe. Representing that cause explicitly makes failures more
diagnosable and makes abstention meaningful.

The v3 suite is intentionally synthetic, but it is broad enough to stress the
mechanism. It shows known-taxonomy improvement, unknown-taxonomy failure,
guarded abstention tradeoffs, active-probe gains, sequential-fixture weakness,
free-control boundaries, and oracle calibration gaps. That is the final shape
of the paper for this batch pass.
